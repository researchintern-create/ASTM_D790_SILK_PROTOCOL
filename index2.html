Test procedure — step by step
Step 01  ·  Preparation
Specimen cutting & dimensions
Cut ≥ 5 rectangular strips from the silk ADM sheet. Width must not exceed span ÷ 4. Depth = material thickness. Opposite sides must be flat and parallel (±0.2 mm).
n ≥ 5 per direction ASTM D5947 dimensions
Step 02  ·  Conditioning
Environmental conditioning
Condition per ASTM D618 Procedure A: 23 ± 2 °C, 50 ± 5% relative humidity, minimum 40 hours. For silk ADM — also prepare hydrated set soaked in PBS 30 min before testing.
23 ± 2 °C Wet state = clinical relevance
Step 03  ·  Setup
Measure dimensions & set span
Measure width (b) and depth (d) to nearest 0.03 mm at the centre of the support span. Set span L = 16 × d. Align loading nose exactly midway between supports. Verify parallelism.
L = 16 × d (±1%) Loading nose ≠ touching specimen yet
Step 04  ·  Setup
Calculate & set crosshead rate R
Calculate R = ZL² / 6d where Z = 0.01 mm/mm/min (Procedure A). Programme the Instron/UTM to this rate. Actual crosshead rate must not differ by more than ±10%.
Z = 0.01 (Procedure A) ±10% tolerance on R
Step 05  ·  Testing
Apply load — record load-deflection
Apply load at rate R. Record simultaneous load P (N) vs deflection D (mm) continuously. Measure deflection by crosshead position (Type I) or deflectometer (Type II — more accurate). Report which method was used.

Detailed notes

Machine setup and compliance correction: Universal testing machines (e.g., Instron 3343, Zwick Z2.5) exhibit drive-system compliance — reported crosshead displacement differs from actual specimen displacement due to load frame stiffness, drive wind-up, and fixture compliance. For accurate flexural modulus of a soft biomaterial like silk ADM, either use a Type II deflectometer (preferred — eliminates compliance error entirely) or perform the machine compliance correction procedure described in Appendix X1 of ASTM D790-17 before reporting EB.

The tangent modulus EB is the slope m of the steepest initial linear region of the load-deflection curve, fitted by hand or via linear regression software. Silk ADM may not show a clearly defined linear (Hookean) region — if so, report the secant modulus at a pre-specified strain (e.g. 2%) and state which method was used. Chord modulus between two fixed strain points (Eq 7) is also acceptable — state the chosen strain interval.

Toe compensation (Annex A1) is mandatory: The initial concave "toe" region of the curve is caused by specimen seating and machine slack — not a material property. Construct a tangent to the steepest initial linear region and extrapolate to the strain axis. The x-intercept (Point B) is the corrected zero-strain point. All strains must be measured from B. Skipping this step will systematically underestimate EB.

Support span-to-depth ratio: 16:1 is standard. Note that for soft, pliable materials like silk ADM where the tensile:shear strength ratio may be low, verify that failure occurs in the outer fibres due to bending moment alone (not shear failure). If shear failure is suspected, increase the span-to-depth ratio to 32:1 or 40:1 and document the change.

Anisotropy — cut and test both orientations: Silk fibroin scaffolds are anisotropic. Flexural modulus and strength will differ significantly in the warp vs weft direction (or longitudinal vs transverse if non-woven). Cut ≥5 specimens in each orientation. Report EB and σfM separately for each direction. This is not required by D790-17 for isotropic plastics but is essential for silk ADM characterisation.

Expected load-deflection curve behaviour: Silk ADM is semi-rigid and hydrophilic. Expect Curve c (Fig. 1 of D790-17) — no break or yield within the 5% strain limit under Procedure A. If this occurs, switch to Procedure B (Z = 0.10 mm/mm/min) and document the switch. If still no break, report the stress at 5% strain as the primary result. This is clinically useful — it tells you the load the scaffold transmits to surrounding tissue at maximum physiological deformation.

Conditioning note: D618 standard conditioning (23 °C, 50% RH) applies to plastics. Silk ADM is implanted in a wet physiological environment. Run a parallel set of specimens in PBS-hydrated state (soak 30 min in PBS at 37 °C before testing, test while wet). Report both dry and wet values. The wet state will typically show a 30–60% reduction in EB vs dry — this is the clinically relevant data point for your breast reconstruction application.

What is this test actually doing? The ASTM D790-17 flexural test physically bends a strip of the silk ADM matrix over two support rods with a single central load — exactly like a beam being bent. As we push down, we record how much force is needed and how much the material deflects. From this simple test, we extract two critical numbers:

  • Flexural modulus (EB, in MPa) — stiffness. How much the material resists bending. A high modulus = stiff. A low modulus = flexible. For breast reconstruction, we need a sweet spot: stiff enough to structurally support the implant, flexible enough to conform to the chest wall contour and not cause patient discomfort.
  • Flexural strength (σfM, in MPa) — how much bending stress the scaffold can survive before it yields or breaks. This sets the safety margin: the scaffold must never fail under physiological loading during daily activity (breathing, movement, pressure).
Why does this test qualify our silk ADM for clinical use? Existing commercial ADMs (AlloDerm, DermACELL, BellaCell HD) have documented flexural stiffness values in the range of 0.28–0.90 N/mm. Our silk ADM's EB from D790-17 lets us directly compare against those benchmarks and claim "our material is mechanically comparable to X" in regulatory submissions and publications.

What if the material doesn't break in the test? This is actually expected — soft biomaterials like silk are elastic and tough. The standard accounts for this: if no break occurs, we stop at 5% strain and report the stress at that point. This is still a valid, publishable result. If we need a stronger signal, we switch to a faster strain rate (Procedure B) and document that in the report.

Timeline and resource estimate: With specimens pre-cut and the Instron set up, a full batch of 5 specimens takes approximately 2–3 hours (including conditioning, setup, testing both dry and PBS-hydrated states). Standard statistical analysis adds another 30 minutes. This test can be completed within a single working day per material batch.

Regulatory relevance: ASTM D790-17 is a recognised international standard (harmonised with ISO 178) and is accepted by CDSCO, FDA, and CE for mechanical characterisation of medical device biomaterials. Results from this test can be directly cited in a 510(k) technical file, PMCF data, or a research publication — which strengthens our IP and clinical validation pipeline.
Silk-specific considerations not covered by ASTM D790-17
💧
Hydration state

Always test in PBS-wet state

ASTM D790-17 conditions specimens at 23 °C / 50% RH (standard plastic conditions). Silk ADM is implanted in a wet, 37 °C physiological environment. Dry silk is 30–60% stiffer than hydrated silk — dry-state results will overestimate the scaffold's in-service stiffness and may mislead clinical decision-making.

Action: Prepare a parallel set of specimens soaked in PBS at 37 °C for 30 minutes. Test while wet. Report dry state results AND wet state results separately. Wet-state EB is the clinically relevant value for all regulatory filings.

Mandatory for clinical correlation
🧵
Anisotropy

Test both fibre orientations

D790-17 tests isotropic plastics in one direction. Silk fibroin scaffolds (woven, knitted, or aligned electrospun) have directionally dependent mechanical properties. Flexural modulus and strength can differ by 2× or more between the fibre direction and the cross-fibre direction.

Action: Cut ≥5 specimens in the longitudinal (along-fibre) direction AND ≥5 in the transverse (cross-fibre) direction. Report EB, σfM, and εfB separately for each orientation. This is essential for understanding how the implant will behave under the directional forces it experiences in vivo.

2 orientation sets required
📉
No-break behaviour

Silk will likely not break at 5% strain

ASTM D790-17 primarily targets plastics that break at small deflections (Curve a). Silk ADM is tough and elastic — it is highly likely to show Curve c behaviour (no yield or break within 5% strain), especially when hydrated. Procedure A alone may not be sufficient.

Action: Run Procedure A first (Z = 0.01 mm/mm/min). If no break or yield within 5% strain, document this result then run Procedure B (Z = 0.10 mm/mm/min) on a fresh set of specimens. Report the stress at 5% strain as the primary flexural result. If benchmarking against commercial ADMs, report flexural modulus as the primary comparator metric since strength comparisons require a common failure mode.

Run Procedure B as fallback
⏱
Time-dependence

Viscoelastic creep under sustained load

Silk fibroin is a viscoelastic biopolymer — its mechanical response is time-dependent. Under a sustained load, the material will continue to deform (creep) beyond the initial elastic response. ASTM D790-17 measures instantaneous load-deflection behaviour only and does not capture long-term creep, which is clinically relevant for a permanent implant support.

Action: Supplement D790-17 with a creep compliance test (ASTM D2990 or ISO 899-1) — apply a constant sub-yield load and record deformation over 100–1000 hours. Report the flexural creep compliance D(t). This data is increasingly expected in breast implant device submissions and differentiates a rigorous characterisation from a basic one.

Supplement with ASTM D2990 creep test
🔁
Fatigue / cyclic loading

In vivo loading is cyclic, not monotonic

D790-17 applies a single monotonic (one-direction, increasing) load until failure or 5% strain. A breast implant support experiences millions of low-amplitude cyclic flexural loads in vivo (breathing: ~12 cycles/min × 525,600 min/year). A scaffold that passes D790-17 could still fail by fatigue under cyclic loading.

Action: Supplement with a cyclic flexural fatigue test at 30–50% of the D790-17 flexural strength. Run at 1–5 Hz to simulate physiological frequency. Report number of cycles to failure and the stress–life (S–N) curve. This is particularly important for silk ADM because silk degrades in vivo — fatigue life may decrease as degradation progresses.

Strongly recommended for regulatory filing
🌡
Temperature sensitivity

Test at 37 °C in addition to 23 °C

ASTM D790-17 tests at room temperature (23 °C). Silk fibroin's mechanical properties are temperature-sensitive — the modulus at 37 °C (body temperature) will be lower than at 23 °C due to increased chain mobility and plasticisation by water. D790-17 results at 23 °C will overestimate in-service stiffness.

Action: Use an environmental chamber or thermal bath fixture to test PBS-hydrated specimens at 37 °C. Report EB at 23 °C (standard, for comparison) AND EB at 37 °C (in-service condition, for clinical relevance). The delta between the two values is the thermal compliance correction factor — document it explicitly in your characterisation report.

Report both 23 °C and 37 °C values
🧬
β-sheet crystallinity

Correlate EB with FTIR / XRD crystallinity index

The flexural modulus of silk fibroin is directly governed by its β-sheet crystallinity content — higher β-sheet fraction (silk II polymorph) = higher stiffness and slower biodegradation. ASTM D790-17 reports a mechanical property but does not identify the root cause of batch-to-batch variability in modulus.

Action: Run FTIR (amide I peak at ~1625 cm⁻¹ for β-sheet) and XRD (peaks at ~9.7° and ~20.7° 2θ) on each specimen batch. Establish a correlation between crystallinity index and EB. This correlation becomes your in-process quality control tool — much faster to run spectroscopy than a full D790 test on every production batch.

Build crystallinity–modulus correlation
📊
Benchmarking strategy

How to compare your results to commercial ADMs

Commercial ADM mechanical data (AlloDerm, DermACELL, BellaCell HD, Strattice) is typically reported as stiffness (N/mm) from probe compression or suture pull-out — not always as flexural modulus (MPa) from a D790-17 test. Direct comparison requires careful normalisation by specimen geometry.

Action: Report EB in MPa (from D790-17) AND convert to apparent stiffness k (N/mm) using the beam bending formula k = 48EI/L³ where I = bd³/12. This allows cross-comparison with literature values. Additionally, run ASTM F1306 (burst strength and suture pull-out) alongside D790-17 so your mechanical characterisation uses the same standards as the predicate device comparisons in the literature.

Run D790 + F1306 in parallel
